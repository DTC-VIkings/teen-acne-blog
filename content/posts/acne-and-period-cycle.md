# Why Your Acne Gets Worse Before Your Period (And What to Do About It)

I used to think my breakouts were random. Like some weeks my skin would look fine, and then out of nowhere I'd wake up with three angry bumps on my chin. It took me way too long to realize this was happening on a schedule.

If you've noticed your acne flaring up about a week before your period, you're not imagining things. There's a hormonal mechanism behind it, and once you understand the timing, you can actually get ahead of it instead of just reacting after the damage is done.

![A teenager marking dates on a calendar](/images/period-calendar-marking.jpg)

## What's Happening Inside Your Body

Your menstrual cycle runs roughly 28 days (though anywhere from 21 to 35 is normal). The part that matters for your skin is what happens in the second half, called the luteal phase. This kicks in after ovulation, around day 14 or so.

During the first half of your cycle, estrogen is the dominant hormone. Estrogen is generally good for skin. It helps keep sebum production lower, supports skin barrier function, and has mild anti-inflammatory properties [1]. This is why many people notice their skin looks its best in the week or two after their period ends.

Then ovulation happens. Progesterone spikes. And here's where the trouble starts.

Progesterone does a few things that are bad news for acne-prone skin. It stimulates your sebaceous glands to produce more oil. It also causes mild swelling of the skin that can compress pore openings, trapping that extra oil inside [2]. Meanwhile, estrogen drops off in the last few days before your period. This shift in the estrogen-to-progesterone ratio is what triggers the breakout.

But progesterone isn't the only player. Your body also produces androgens throughout your cycle, and there's a relative increase in androgen activity during the late luteal phase because there's less estrogen around to counterbalance them. Androgens are the big sebum drivers. They directly increase oil production and make sebum thicker and stickier [3].

## Why It's Always the Chin and Jawline

This is something I find genuinely interesting. Period-related breakouts almost always cluster along the lower face: chin, jawline, sometimes the neck. There's a reason for that.

The skin on your lower face has a higher density of androgen receptors compared to your forehead or cheeks [4]. So when androgen activity increases in that pre-period window, these areas get hit hardest. The pores there are also generally larger and produce more sebum at baseline.

If your breakouts are mostly on your upper face or spread evenly everywhere, hormones might still be a factor, but other things are probably contributing too. The chin-and-jaw pattern is the classic hormonal signature.

## The Timeline: 7 to 10 Days Before

Most studies on menstrual acne have found that breakouts peak in the week before menstruation begins, with some people noticing changes as early as 10 days out [5]. A study published in the *Archives of Dermatology* found that 63% of acne-prone women experienced premenstrual flares, and the timing was consistent enough to predict [6].

This matters because it gives you a window. If you know when your period is coming, you know roughly when your skin is going to start acting up. And that means you can adjust your routine before the breakout forms, not after.

## A Pre-Period Skincare Strategy That Actually Works

I think the biggest mistake people make with period acne is treating it reactively. By the time you see the pimple, the clog formed days ago. Here's what I'd do instead.

**Starting 7-10 days before your expected period:**

**Increase your active treatment frequency.** If you normally use benzoyl peroxide or adapalene (Differin) a few times a week, bump it to nightly during this window. Adapalene is especially good here because it prevents the comedones (clogged pores) that become inflammatory pimples a few days later [7]. You need it in your skin before the clog forms.

**Add a salicylic acid wash or leave-on treatment.** Salicylic acid is oil-soluble, which means it can actually get inside your pores and break up the sebum plugs that progesterone is encouraging. A 2% salicylic acid cleanser used once daily during your luteal phase can make a noticeable difference.

**Don't skip your moisturizer.** I know it feels counterintuitive when your skin is getting oilier, but stripping moisture from your skin triggers more oil production. Use a lightweight, non-comedogenic moisturizer. Something with niacinamide is a smart pick since niacinamide helps regulate sebum.

**Spot treat early.** If you feel a bump forming under the skin (that deep, painful kind), hit it with a 2.5% benzoyl peroxide spot treatment immediately. Don't wait to see if it goes away. It won't.

**Consider a clay mask once or twice during the week before.** A kaolin or bentonite clay mask can absorb some of the excess oil without being harsh. This isn't going to prevent breakouts on its own, but it helps manage the surface oil that contributes to clogged pores.

## Birth Control and Acne: A Brief Note for Older Teens

If you're 16+ and dealing with persistent hormonal acne, it's worth knowing that certain combined oral contraceptives (the ones with both estrogen and progestin) are FDA-approved for acne treatment. Brands like Ortho Tri-Cyclen, Yaz, and Beyaz have clinical data showing they reduce acne lesions by stabilizing hormonal fluctuations throughout the cycle [8].

This works because the pill provides a steady level of estrogen that prevents the natural progesterone-dominant phase from spiking your oil production. It also increases sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG), which binds free testosterone and reduces androgen-driven breakouts.

I want to be honest about this though: birth control isn't a casual decision, and it comes with its own side effects and considerations. It's a conversation to have with your doctor or dermatologist, not something to start because a blog told you to. But if your period acne is severe and not responding to topical treatments, it's a legitimate medical option, not just a reproductive one.

One thing to watch out for: progestin-only methods (like the mini-pill, hormonal IUDs, or the implant) can sometimes make acne worse because they don't provide the estrogen counterbalance. If you're already on one of these and your acne got worse after starting, that connection is worth exploring with your doctor.

## Track Your Cycle. Seriously.

This is the most practical advice in this entire post. Start tracking your cycle if you aren't already.

You don't need anything fancy. A basic period tracking app works. Clue, Flo, or even just marking the dates on your phone calendar. The goal is to figure out your personal pattern. After 2-3 months of tracking both your period start date and when breakouts appear, you'll probably see the connection clearly.

Some people break out exactly 7 days before. Some it's more like 4-5 days. Your pattern might be slightly different from the textbook average, and knowing your specific timing lets you fine-tune when to start ramping up your skincare.

I'd also note: if your cycle is very irregular (varying by more than 7-8 days month to month), that itself can be connected to your acne. Irregular cycles sometimes point to hormonal imbalances that need medical attention.

## When Period Acne Might Be PCOS

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) affects somewhere around 6-12% of women of reproductive age [9]. It's one of the most common hormonal disorders in teens, and acne is one of its hallmark symptoms.

Here's when to think about PCOS rather than normal menstrual acne:

- Your periods are very irregular or infrequent (fewer than 8 per year)
- Your acne is severe, persistent, and doesn't respond well to standard treatments
- You're also experiencing excess hair growth on your face, chest, or back
- You have significant hair thinning on your scalp
- You gained weight that's been hard to lose, particularly around your midsection

PCOS-related acne tends to be more persistent and severe than typical premenstrual breakouts because the androgen levels are elevated throughout the cycle, not just in the pre-period window. The acne is still concentrated on the lower face and jawline, but it often doesn't clear up between periods the way typical menstrual acne does.

If any of this sounds familiar, bring it up with your doctor. PCOS is diagnosed with blood work and sometimes an ultrasound. It's very treatable, but it does require a different approach than regular period acne. Treatments like spironolactone (which blocks androgens at the skin level) can be very effective for PCOS-related acne [10].

## Key Takeaways

1. **The progesterone-estrogen shift in your luteal phase (7-10 days before your period) is what triggers breakouts.** It's not random and it's not your fault.
2. **Chin and jawline breakouts are the classic hormonal pattern** because those areas have more androgen receptors.
3. **Start treating your skin before the breakout forms.** Increase adapalene or benzoyl peroxide use starting a week before your expected period.
4. **Track your cycle for 2-3 months** to learn your personal breakout timeline.
5. **If your acne is severe, persistent, or paired with irregular periods,** talk to a doctor about possible PCOS or hormonal treatment options.

## Bottom Line

Period acne is one of the most predictable forms of acne, which actually makes it one of the most manageable. The hormonal shift is going to happen every month. You can't stop it. But you can prepare your skin for it, reduce the severity, and stop feeling blindsided every 28 days. Track your cycle, adjust your routine during the luteal phase, and save the heavy-duty spot treatments for when you actually need them. If it's still not enough, a dermatologist can help you explore hormonal options that go beyond what skincare alone can do.
