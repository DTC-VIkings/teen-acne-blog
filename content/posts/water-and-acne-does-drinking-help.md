# Does Drinking More Water Clear Acne? The Truth Is Boring

You've heard it. I've heard it. Every person with acne has heard it from someone who has never had acne: "Have you tried drinking more water?"

It's the universal acne advice. It costs nothing. It sounds scientific enough to feel true. It gives the advice-giver a warm sense of having contributed. And the evidence behind it is... almost nonexistent.

I know. I'm sorry. I wanted this to be a different article too.

![A teenager drinking water from a reusable bottle](/images/teen-drinking-water-bottle.jpg)

## The Claim

The basic argument goes something like this: your body is 60% water. Your skin is the largest organ. If you're dehydrated, your skin suffers. Dehydrated skin produces more oil to compensate. More oil means more clogged pores. More clogged pores means more acne. Therefore, drinking water clears acne.

It sounds logical. Each individual step seems reasonable. The problem is that when you look for actual clinical evidence connecting water intake to acne outcomes, you find almost nothing. And what little exists doesn't say what people want it to say.

## What the Research Actually Shows

I spent a genuinely embarrassing amount of time searching PubMed for studies directly linking water consumption to acne. Here's what I found:

Pretty much nothing.

There are no large randomized controlled trials testing whether increased water intake improves acne. Zero. None. This is not a case where the evidence is mixed or inconclusive. The studies simply haven't been done. When a dermatology textbook mentions water and acne, it's usually one sentence that amounts to "adequate hydration is generally recommended for skin health" with no citation (Zaenglein et al., 2016).

There is one small study from 2015 published in *Clinical, Cosmetic and Investigational Dermatology* that looked at skin hydration and dietary water intake. It found that higher water intake was associated with improved skin hydration measurements, particularly in people who were drinking less than the recommended amount at baseline (Palma et al., 2015). But the study didn't measure acne. It measured skin hydration using a device pressed against the forearm. Those are different things.

Another study in the *International Journal of Cosmetic Science* found that drinking 2.25 liters of water daily for four weeks increased skin thickness and density as measured by ultrasound, but again, did not look at acne (Mac-Mary et al., 2006). Thicker, denser skin is not the same as clearer skin.

That's about the extent of it. The entire "drink water for clear skin" recommendation is built on plausible-sounding reasoning rather than direct evidence. This doesn't mean water definitely doesn't help with acne. It means nobody has rigorously tested it, which is a different statement. But it also means anyone telling you water will clear your skin is making a promise the science hasn't backed up.

## What Hydration Actually Does for Your Skin

Okay, so water won't magically unclog your pores. But that doesn't mean hydration is irrelevant to skin health. Let me give credit where it's due.

### Supports the Skin Barrier

Your skin has an outermost layer called the stratum corneum. Think of it as a brick wall: skin cells are the bricks, and lipids (fats) are the mortar holding them together. This barrier keeps moisture in and irritants out.

When this barrier is compromised, moisture escapes (a process called transepidermal water loss), and your skin becomes more vulnerable to bacteria, pollution, and irritation. Adequate systemic hydration supports this barrier, though the effect is modest compared to what you apply topically. A moisturizer on your face does more for your skin barrier than an extra glass of water. That's just how skin works.

Research published in the *Journal of Biological Chemistry* has shown that aquaporins, the protein channels that transport water through cells, play a role in skin hydration and barrier function (Hara-Chikuma & Verkman, 2008). But again, this research is about skin hydration generally, not acne specifically.

### General Health and Healing

Water is obviously necessary for basically every bodily function. Your kidneys need it, your cells need it, your blood needs it. If you're significantly dehydrated, your body prioritizes vital organs over skin repair. Wound healing slows down. Inflammation can be harder to resolve.

But here's the thing: you'd have to be genuinely dehydrated for this to matter. Not "I forgot to bring my water bottle to school" dehydrated. More like "I've been hiking in the desert without water for a day" dehydrated. The level of dehydration that would meaningfully impair skin function is the level where you'd have much bigger problems than acne.

Most teenagers in developed countries are not walking around clinically dehydrated. A 2015 CDC report found that while some kids aren't drinking as much water as recommended, actual clinical dehydration is uncommon (Kenney et al., 2015). If you're drinking when you're thirsty and your urine isn't dark yellow, you're probably fine.

## The Dehydrated-Skin-Overproduces-Oil Theory

This is the most repeated claim, and I'll admit it has a kernel of truth buried under layers of exaggeration.

The theory: when skin is dehydrated, it senses the lack of moisture and compensates by ramping up sebum production. Since excess oil is one of the four factors that cause acne, dehydrated skin could theoretically contribute to breakouts through this pathway.

There is some basis for this. Dehydrated skin and oily skin are not mutually exclusive conditions. You can absolutely have skin that lacks water content while simultaneously producing too much oil. Dermatologists see this combination regularly.

However, the leap from "dehydrated skin can be oily" to "drinking more water will reduce oil production and clear acne" skips about five steps, each of which is unproven:

1. That the dehydration is caused by insufficient water intake rather than a damaged skin barrier, dry environment, or harsh products
2. That drinking water meaningfully increases hydration specifically in the skin of the face
3. That this hydration increase would be enough to reduce sebum production
4. That the reduction in sebum would be enough to prevent clogged pores
5. That preventing those specific clogged pores would result in visibly less acne

Each step is plausible. None of them has been demonstrated in a clinical setting. Stringing plausible-but-unproven steps together doesn't give you a proven conclusion. It gives you a hypothesis that sounds good at parties.

The sebaceous glands are primarily controlled by androgens, not by hydration status. A 2016 review in *Dermato-Endocrinology* detailed the hormonal regulation of sebum production and didn't identify hydration as a meaningful variable (Makrantonaki et al., 2011). Your oil glands are listening to your hormones, not your water bottle.

## What Water Definitely Does NOT Do

Let me clear up a few specific claims that I see repeated constantly.

### "Water Flushes Toxins That Cause Acne"

This is not how any of this works. Your liver and kidneys handle toxins. They do a remarkable job of it regardless of whether you drink six glasses of water or eight. Drinking extra water does not speed up "detoxification" because that's not a real bottleneck in a healthy body.

The word "toxins" in skincare advice is almost always a red flag. If someone can't name the specific toxin they're claiming water will flush, they're repeating something they heard rather than something they know.

### "Water Clears Your Pores"

Pores don't get cleared from the inside by drinking water. A clogged pore has a physical plug made of dead skin cells, sebum, and sometimes bacteria. Water you drink goes to your bloodstream, your cells, your kidneys. It does not travel to individual pores and dissolve blockages. That would be like pouring water into your car's gas tank and expecting it to wash the windshield.

### "Dehydration Causes Acne"

As discussed, there's no clinical evidence for this. Severe dehydration causes all sorts of problems, none of which are acne. The symptoms of actual dehydration include dizziness, dark urine, confusion, and rapid heartbeat. Not pimples.

## So Why Does Everyone Recommend It?

I have a theory about this. Actually, a few theories.

**It's safe advice.** Nobody is going to get hurt drinking reasonable amounts of water. A doctor or well-meaning relative can recommend it without any risk of side effects. Compare that to recommending a medication or even a specific skincare product, which could cause reactions. Water is the zero-liability recommendation.

**It feels proactive.** When someone you care about has acne, you want to help. Suggesting water gives both parties something to do. It's actionable, free, and immediate. The fact that it probably won't clear acne doesn't diminish how satisfying it is to feel like you're doing something.

**Survivorship bias.** People who started drinking more water AND changed their skincare routine AND hit a natural hormonal shift see their acne improve, then attribute it to the water because that's the change they were most aware of. Nobody runs a controlled experiment on themselves.

**It aligns with general health messaging.** "Drink more water" is good general health advice. It just gets stretched into specific medical claims it can't support.

## The Boring Truth

Here is the honest, unsexy, boring conclusion: you should drink water because water is good for you. You need it to live. Your skin, like every other organ, functions better when you're adequately hydrated than when you're not.

But drinking more water is not going to clear your acne. If you have acne, you have a skin condition driven by hormones, genetics, bacteria, and inflammation. Treating it requires treatments that actually target those factors: retinoids, benzoyl peroxide, salicylic acid, antibiotics, hormonal therapies, or isotretinoin in severe cases.

I know this is less exciting than "just drink 8 glasses a day and watch your skin glow." But I'd rather tell you something boring and true than something exciting and unsupported. You deserve to spend your time and energy on interventions that actually work rather than waiting for water to do something it can't.

The 8-glasses-a-day number, by the way, has its own questionable origins. A 2002 review in the *American Journal of Physiology* traced it to a 1945 government recommendation that was probably misinterpreted and has never been validated by clinical research (Valtin, 2002). Drink when you're thirsty. That's the evidence-based recommendation. Your body has a perfectly functional thirst mechanism.

## What to Do Instead

If you're reading this because you have acne and you've been told water is the answer, here's what I'd actually suggest:

**Use a gentle, non-comedogenic moisturizer.** If your skin is dehydrated, applying hydration directly to the skin is far more effective than drinking it. Look for ingredients like hyaluronic acid, glycerin, or ceramides. This actually addresses the skin barrier issue that the water theory is trying to get at.

**Focus on proven acne treatments.** Benzoyl peroxide kills acne-causing bacteria. Salicylic acid helps unclog pores. Retinoids normalize skin cell turnover. These target the actual mechanisms of acne.

**See a dermatologist if over-the-counter treatments aren't working after 8-12 weeks.** There are prescription options that work, and you don't need to struggle through this alone.

**And yes, drink water.** Just do it because it's a basic human need, not because you think it'll fix your skin.

## Key Takeaways

- **There are zero clinical trials directly linking water intake to acne improvement.** The recommendation is based on plausible reasoning, not evidence.
- **Water supports general skin health and barrier function**, but the effect is modest and not specific to acne.
- **The "dehydrated skin overproduces oil" theory has some basis** but the chain from "drink more water" to "less acne" has too many unproven links.
- **Water does not flush toxins, clear pores, or detoxify your skin.** Those aren't real mechanisms.
- **Topical moisturizers hydrate your skin more effectively than drinking extra water.** If dry skin is contributing to your problems, address it directly.

## The Bottom Line

Drinking water is good for you. It is not an acne treatment. I realize that's a disappointing answer, and I genuinely wish the science told a different story. But the most helpful thing I can do is point you toward treatments that have actual evidence behind them rather than let you spend months waiting for your water bottle to fix a medical condition. Drink your water. Also use benzoyl peroxide. The boring combination of hydration and real treatments will serve you much better than either one alone.
