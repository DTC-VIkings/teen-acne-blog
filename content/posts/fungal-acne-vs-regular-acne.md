# Fungal Acne vs. Regular Acne: How to Tell the Difference

![Comparison of fungal acne bumps versus regular acne pimples](/images/fungal-vs-regular-comparison.jpg)

I spent about four months treating what I thought was stubborn forehead acne before someone pointed out it might not be acne at all. Same tiny bumps, week after week, not getting better despite salicylic acid and benzoyl peroxide. Turns out I was treating the wrong condition entirely.

Fungal acne is one of the most commonly misdiagnosed skin issues in teenagers. The name itself is misleading because it's not acne. It's a yeast infection of the hair follicles. And if you treat it like regular acne, you'll either get nowhere or make it worse.

## It's not actually acne

What people call "fungal acne" is technically *pityrosporum folliculitis* (some sources now call it *Malassezia folliculitis*). The cause is a yeast called *Malassezia* that naturally lives on everyone's skin. It's part of your normal skin flora. The problem starts when it overgrows inside hair follicles, triggering inflammation [1].

Regular acne (acne vulgaris) is caused by a completely different process. Excess sebum and dead skin cells clog a pore, the bacterium *Cutibacterium acnes* feeds on the trapped oil, your immune system reacts, and you get a pimple. The clogging, the bacteria, and the immune response are all different from what happens in fungal folliculitis.

This distinction matters because the treatments target different organisms. Antibacterial products don't kill yeast. Antifungal products don't kill bacteria. Using the wrong one is like putting diesel in a gasoline engine.

## How to tell the difference

Here's where it gets tricky: they can look pretty similar at first glance. But there are reliable differences if you know what to look for.

![A teenager examining uniform small bumps on their forehead](/images/uniform-bumps-forehead.jpg)

**Fungal acne bumps are uniform.** This is the biggest giveaway. With regular acne, you get a mix of different lesion types: blackheads, whiteheads, small red bumps, larger inflamed pimples, maybe a cyst or two. They're different sizes and at different stages. Fungal acne looks like someone stamped the same tiny bump across your skin. They're almost all the same size (usually 1-2mm), the same color, and at the same stage of development [2].

**It itches.** Regular acne can be sore or tender, but it doesn't usually itch. Fungal acne frequently does. If your breakout is itchy, that should immediately make you wonder about fungal involvement. One study found that pruritus (itching) was present in about 80% of pityrosporum folliculitis cases [3].

**Location tends to be different.** Fungal acne favors the forehead, chest, upper back, and shoulders. These are areas where *Malassezia* thrives because they tend to be warm, sweaty, and oily. Regular acne can show up there too, but if your breakout is concentrated on the forehead or trunk with minimal involvement of the cheeks and jawline, fungal acne should be on your radar.

**It doesn't respond to regular acne treatment.** This is often how people figure it out. You've been using benzoyl peroxide or salicylic acid or adapalene for weeks, and those identical bumps are still there. Maybe they've even gotten worse. Standard acne treatments don't affect *Malassezia* yeast, so if nothing is working, that's a clue.

**You don't get blackheads or cystic lesions.** Fungal folliculitis produces papules (small raised bumps) and sometimes small pustules (bumps with a white center). It doesn't produce open comedones (blackheads), closed comedones, or deep cystic nodules. If you're seeing a mix of blackheads and deep painful bumps alongside the small uniform ones, the uniform ones might still be fungal, but you likely have both conditions happening at the same time. Which is entirely possible and actually pretty common.

## Why it gets misdiagnosed so often

Dermatologists misdiagnose this too. A retrospective study found that pityrosporum folliculitis is frequently misdiagnosed as acne vulgaris, with patients seeing an average of two physicians before getting the correct diagnosis [4]. If trained dermatologists get it wrong, it's no surprise that teens trying to self-diagnose from the mirror get confused.

Part of the problem is that the two conditions overlap. A lot of people have both regular acne and fungal folliculitis simultaneously. The acne gets diagnosed and treated, and when those specific small bumps don't clear up, everyone assumes the acne treatment just isn't working well enough. So they increase the dose or switch products when what they really need is an antifungal.

Another problem is that some acne treatments actively feed *Malassezia*. Many moisturizers, sunscreens, and even some acne products contain fatty acids and esters that the yeast uses as food. So your skincare routine might be treating your acne while inadvertently feeding your fungal folliculitis.

## What triggers it

*Malassezia* yeast is always on your skin. The question is what makes it overgrow. Several things:

**Sweat and humidity.** Warm, moist conditions are where this yeast thrives. If your fungal acne gets worse in summer, after workouts, or when you live somewhere humid, that's consistent. A study in the *British Journal of Dermatology* confirmed that hot, humid climates significantly increase pityrosporum folliculitis incidence [5].

**Antibiotics.** This is a common one that catches people off guard. Oral antibiotics, like the ones prescribed for regular acne (doxycycline, minocycline), kill bacteria but leave yeast untouched. With bacterial competition removed, *Malassezia* can overgrow. I've seen people start antibiotics for acne, watch their typical pimples improve, and then develop a new crop of tiny uniform bumps they've never had before. That's often fungal folliculitis filling the space the bacteria left behind [6].

**Occlusive products.** Heavy creams, oils, and products with certain fatty acids (especially oleic acid, lauric acid, and their esters) can feed *Malassezia*. Some popular skincare ingredients that are technically comedogenic for yeast include polysorbate, certain fatty alcohols, and some forms of squalane. This is why people sometimes say their skincare routine made their "acne" worse, when what actually happened is the products triggered or worsened fungal folliculitis.

**Tight clothing and sports gear.** Anything that traps sweat against skin for extended periods, like synthetic athletic wear, helmets, or backpack straps, creates the environment *Malassezia* loves.

**Immunosuppression.** This is less common in otherwise healthy teens but worth mentioning. Anything that suppresses the immune system can allow yeast overgrowth.

## How to treat it

The good news is that fungal acne responds well to treatment once you know what you're dealing with. The bad news is that most of the effective treatments aren't marketed for "acne."

![Antifungal treatment products on a bathroom shelf](/images/antifungal-products-shelf.jpg)

**Ketoconazole shampoo as a face wash.** This sounds weird, I know. But Nizoral (2% ketoconazole shampoo, available over the counter) is one of the most effective topical treatments for pityrosporum folliculitis. You apply it to the affected area, leave it on for 3-5 minutes like a mask, then rinse. Doing this daily for 2-3 weeks and then a few times a week for maintenance works for a lot of people [7]. The active ingredient kills *Malassezia* on contact.

**Zinc pyrithione.** Found in dandruff shampoos like Head & Shoulders and in some bar soaps (the Vanicream Z-Bar is one). Zinc pyrithione is antifungal and can be used as a face or body wash. It's gentler than ketoconazole and works well for maintenance once you've gotten things under control.

**Selenium sulfide.** Another dandruff shampoo ingredient (Selsun Blue). Same concept: apply, leave on a few minutes, rinse. Less studied specifically for folliculitis than ketoconazole, but many dermatologists recommend it.

**Oral antifungals for stubborn cases.** If topical treatments don't work after a month or so, a dermatologist can prescribe oral fluconazole or itraconazole. These are more effective for widespread or deep folliculitis. A study in the *Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology* found that oral itraconazole produced clearance in about 90% of patients with pityrosporum folliculitis [8].

**Rethink your products.** Check your moisturizer, sunscreen, and makeup for ingredients that feed *Malassezia*. There are online databases (the "folliculitis scout" is a popular one) where you can paste an ingredient list and check for fungal-acne triggers. Switch to products labeled "fungal acne safe" or stick to mineral sunscreens and simple moisturizers with minimal ingredient lists.

## Why regular acne products make it worse

This is the part that trips people up. You have bumps on your forehead, so you add more salicylic acid, more benzoyl peroxide, maybe a clay mask. The bumps don't budge. So you try stronger products, more often, and now your skin is irritated AND you still have the bumps.

Here's what happens. Benzoyl peroxide and most acne-targeted antibiotics are useless against yeast. Meanwhile, the aggressive treatment strips your skin barrier. A damaged skin barrier actually makes it easier for *Malassezia* to cause inflammation. And then people pile on rich, repairing moisturizers that often contain the fatty acids and esters the yeast feeds on.

It becomes a cycle: treat aggressively, damage barrier, repair with products yeast loves, yeast thrives, bumps persist, treat more aggressively. I've seen people stuck in this loop for months.

The fix is recognizing the pattern and stopping. Scale back on the acne actives, add an antifungal treatment, and use a simple moisturizer that doesn't feed yeast.

## When to see a dermatologist

Self-diagnosing fungal acne from photos on Reddit is risky. The gold standard diagnosis involves a KOH preparation, where a dermatologist scrapes a tiny sample from a bump, puts it under a microscope with potassium hydroxide, and looks for yeast clusters. It takes about 10 minutes and is definitive [9].

See a dermatologist if:

- Your bumps are uniform and small and haven't responded to 6-8 weeks of standard acne treatment
- The affected area is itchy
- You recently finished a course of oral antibiotics and developed new bumps
- You suspect you have both regular acne and fungal acne (this is actually the most common scenario, and it needs a combined treatment approach)
- Topical antifungals helped initially but the bumps keep coming back

A dermatologist can confirm whether it's fungal folliculitis, regular acne, or both, and build a treatment plan that addresses all of it instead of accidentally making one condition worse while treating the other.

## Can you have both at the same time?

Yes, and a lot of people do. The forehead bumps are fungal. The chin breakouts are bacterial. The cheek pimples could be either. Having both is probably more common than having fungal folliculitis alone, because the same oily, acne-prone skin that grows *C. acnes* bacteria also provides a good environment for *Malassezia* overgrowth [10].

If you have both, the treatment approach is layered. Antifungal washes for the fungal component, traditional acne actives for the bacterial component, and careful product selection that doesn't feed the yeast while still treating the acne. It's manageable, but it's harder to do on your own. This is where a dermatologist really earns their fee.

## Key takeaways

1. **Fungal acne isn't acne.** It's a yeast overgrowth (*Malassezia*) in hair follicles that looks like acne but needs different treatment.
2. **Uniform small bumps that itch and don't respond to regular acne treatment** are the hallmark signs. Regular acne produces varied lesion types and doesn't typically itch.
3. **Sweat, antibiotics, humidity, and occlusive products** are the most common triggers for fungal folliculitis in teens.
4. **Antifungal products work. Regular acne products don't.** Ketoconazole shampoo, zinc pyrithione, and selenium sulfide are the go-to topical treatments.
5. **See a dermatologist for confirmation** rather than self-diagnosing. A KOH prep is quick, cheap, and gives you a definitive answer.

## Bottom line

If you've been fighting tiny, uniform, itchy bumps on your forehead or chest that refuse to respond to every acne product you throw at them, stop throwing acne products at them. There's a real chance it's fungal folliculitis, not acne, and the treatment is completely different. A dandruff shampoo used as a face wash might clear up what months of salicylic acid couldn't. Get it confirmed by a dermatologist if you can, check your skincare products for yeast-feeding ingredients, and stop assuming that every bump on your face is the same condition. Sometimes the reason nothing works is that you're solving the wrong problem.
