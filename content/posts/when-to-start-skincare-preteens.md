# When Should Preteens Start a Skincare Routine?

Your 10-year-old wants a 7-step skincare routine. She's seen it on TikTok. There's a serum involved, possibly retinol, definitely something with "glow" in the name. She's asking for Drunk Elephant for her birthday.

Meanwhile, you're standing in the bathroom remembering that your own skincare routine at 10 was whatever bar of soap was next to the sink.

Both extremes are wrong, and figuring out where the middle ground actually sits is harder than it should be. Skincare companies aren't going to tell you "your kid doesn't need this yet," because they want the sale. And social media isn't going to tell you that a preteen with retinol is doing more harm than good, because that doesn't get engagement.

So here's what I think, based on the actual dermatology research and a fair amount of common sense.

![A preteen girl looking curiously at skincare products](/images/preteen-curious-products.jpg)

## The Signs It's Time to Start

There isn't a single magic age. Kids develop at different rates, and skincare readiness is tied to biological changes, not calendar birthdays. That said, there are concrete signals that your child's skin is changing and a basic routine would be useful.

**Oily skin, especially in the T-zone.** When the forehead and nose start looking shiny by mid-afternoon, that's sebaceous glands waking up in response to adrenal androgens. This typically starts between ages 8-11 as part of adrenarche, the hormonal process that precedes full puberty (Oberfield et al., 2011). A cleanser becomes useful at this point because water alone doesn't remove excess sebum effectively.

**First blackheads or whiteheads.** Small comedones on the nose are often the earliest visible sign. A study in *Pediatric Dermatology* found that 28% of children aged 9-11 already had identifiable acne lesions (Lucky et al., 1994). If you're noticing tiny dark dots on your kid's nose, that's sebum oxidizing in open pores. It's normal and a reasonable trigger to introduce cleansing.

**Body odor starting.** This seems unrelated to skincare, but it's driven by the same hormonal shift. When a child starts needing deodorant, their skin's oil production is changing too. Think of it as the body's way of signaling that basic hygiene is leveling up.

**Skin feeling "different."** Some kids will tell you directly that their face feels different, oilier, rougher, like something changed. Trust that. They know their own skin better than anyone.

If none of these signs are present, there's no reason to push skincare. A 9-year-old with dry, clear skin doesn't need a cleanser beyond whatever they use in the bath. Starting too early doesn't build good habits; it just creates unnecessary complexity.

## Age 9-11 Is Normal (and Earlier Isn't Alarming)

I want to normalize this because I get the sense many parents feel panicked when their 9 or 10-year-old shows signs of skin changes. "Isn't that too young?"

No. Adrenarche begins on average around age 6-8, with visible skin effects (increased oiliness, early comedones) typically appearing between 8-11. Girls tend to see these changes earlier than boys. The process is driven by adrenal androgens, primarily DHEA and DHEA-S, which are separate from the gonadal hormones of full puberty (Oberfield et al., 2011).

The only scenario where very early skin changes warrant medical attention is if a child under 8 is developing significant acne alongside other signs of precocious puberty, like breast development in girls before age 8 or testicular enlargement in boys before age 9. That pattern can indicate an underlying hormonal issue and should be evaluated by a pediatric endocrinologist (Eichenfield et al., 2013).

But a 9-year-old with some oiliness and a few blackheads on their nose? Completely unremarkable from a medical standpoint. Their body is doing exactly what it's supposed to do.

## Start Simple: Cleanser + Moisturizer. That's It.

This is the part I feel strongly about. The starting routine for a preteen should be two products. Maybe three if you count sunscreen, which I do think is a good habit to build early but isn't strictly about acne.

![A simple 2-product routine for young skin](/images/simple-preteen-routine.jpg)

**Step 1: Gentle cleanser.** Fragrance-free, non-medicated, designed for sensitive skin. The goal is removing excess oil and daily grime without stripping the skin barrier. Preteen skin has a thinner stratum corneum (the outermost protective layer) than adult skin, and the barrier function is still maturing (Stamatas et al., 2010). Harsh cleansers can damage this barrier, leading to dryness, irritation, and paradoxically, increased oil production as the skin tries to compensate.

Good options: CeraVe Hydrating Facial Cleanser, Vanicream Gentle Facial Cleanser, La Roche-Posay Toleriane Hydrating Gentle Cleanser. All of these are $10-15 and last months.

**Step 2: Moisturizer.** Yes, even if their skin is oily. I know this seems counterintuitive. But oil production and skin hydration are different mechanisms. A lightweight, non-comedogenic moisturizer keeps the skin barrier healthy without contributing to breakouts. Research shows that using a moisturizer alongside acne treatments improves outcomes and reduces irritation (Draelos, 2012).

CeraVe PM Facial Moisturizing Lotion or Vanicream Daily Facial Moisturizer are both solid, boring, effective choices.

**Step 3 (optional): Sunscreen.** SPF 30+, broad spectrum, non-comedogenic. This is more about building a lifelong habit than treating acne, though it matters if they ever progress to retinoid use.

That's the complete preteen routine. Twice daily. Takes about 90 seconds.

**When to add a medicated product:** If after 6-8 weeks of consistent cleansing and moisturizing, breakouts are increasing or not improving, adding a salicylic acid cleanser (2%) is a reasonable next step. Salicylic acid is oil-soluble, which means it can penetrate into pores and help dissolve the sebum and dead cell buildup that causes comedones. It's the gentlest acne-active ingredient and appropriate for preteens in most cases.

What I'd hold off on until age 12-13 at the earliest, and even then only for persistent acne: adapalene (Differin), benzoyl peroxide leave-on treatments, or anything prescription-strength.

## Don't Introduce Actives Too Early

This is where I get a bit fired up, because I've seen the damage that premature use of active ingredients causes.

Retinoids, chemical exfoliants (glycolic acid, lactic acid), vitamin C serums, benzoyl peroxide leave-on treatments. These are all effective products. For adults. For teenagers with established acne. They are not appropriate for a 10-year-old's skin in most cases.

Here's why. Active ingredients work by altering skin cell behavior: speeding up turnover, killing bacteria, dissolving dead cells, suppressing oil production. Preteen skin is already turning over rapidly (children have faster natural cell turnover rates than adults). Adding exfoliating acids to skin that's already shedding normally doesn't help. It disrupts the barrier, causes irritation, and can trigger a reactive increase in oil production that makes breakouts worse (Del Rosso & Levin, 2011).

Retinoids specifically are a concern. Adapalene is FDA-approved for ages 12 and up. Tretinoin is typically not recommended for children under 12. Using these products on younger skin without medical guidance can cause significant dryness, peeling, and photosensitivity in a child who probably isn't going to reliably apply sunscreen every day.

A pediatric dermatologist I've read put it well: treating the skin a child will have in three years, rather than the skin they have now, is bad medicine. Match the treatment to the current condition.

## The TikTok Skincare Problem

I need to talk about this because it's genuinely becoming a clinical issue.

Dermatologists across the US have reported a noticeable increase in children aged 8-12 presenting with contact dermatitis, chemical burns, and irritant reactions from inappropriate skincare products. The AAD addressed this directly in 2024, noting that social media-driven skincare trends were leading to preteen misuse of products designed for adult skin (AAD, 2024).

The specific pattern looks like this: a 10-year-old watches skincare content on TikTok or YouTube. The creator, usually in their 20s or 30s, demonstrates a routine involving retinol, AHAs, vitamin C, multiple serums, and maybe a chemical peel. The child asks for these products. The parent, wanting to be supportive or not knowing any better, buys them. Within weeks, the child's skin is red, peeling, irritated, and breaking out worse than before.

I don't blame the kids. They're doing what kids have always done: imitating older people they admire. And I don't entirely blame the parents; most of them don't have the dermatological knowledge to know that a product safe for a 28-year-old isn't safe for a 10-year-old.

I do blame the skincare industry a little. Brands like Drunk Elephant and The Ordinary have massive followings among preteens, and while their products are well-formulated for their intended audience, their marketing doesn't do much to discourage use by children. Drunk Elephant did eventually release a statement saying their products are designed for "all ages," which I think is irresponsible.

**What parents can do:**

Have a direct conversation about why skincare content created by adults isn't meant for kids. Use the car analogy: a 16-year-old can drive, but that doesn't mean a 10-year-old should. Their skin is different, and what's safe for older skin can damage younger skin.

If your child is interested in skincare, channel that interest productively. Let them pick out their gentle cleanser and moisturizer. Make the routine theirs. The desire to take care of their skin isn't bad; it just needs to be directed toward age-appropriate products.

Set boundaries around specific ingredients. No retinoids, no AHAs/BHAs above 2%, no vitamin C serums, no chemical peels. These aren't opinions; they're based on what pediatric dermatologists recommend for this age group.

## What's Appropriate at Each Stage

Since parents keep asking me for a roadmap, here's my best attempt at one. Every child is different, and this isn't medical advice, but it reflects what the research and clinical guidelines support.

**Ages 8-10 (if skin changes are starting):**
- Gentle, fragrance-free cleanser once daily (evening)
- Moisturizer
- That's it

**Ages 10-11 (oilier skin, some blackheads):**
- Gentle cleanser twice daily
- Moisturizer twice daily
- Sunscreen daily
- Salicylic acid cleanser (2%) can replace the gentle cleanser if breakouts increase

**Ages 12-13 (breakouts becoming regular):**
- Medicated cleanser (salicylic acid or low-concentration benzoyl peroxide wash)
- Moisturizer
- Sunscreen
- Adapalene 0.1% every other night if breakouts persist (start slowly)

**Ages 14+ (active acne):**
- Full acne routine with active ingredients as appropriate
- Dermatologist consultation if OTC products aren't working after 12 weeks

The progression should be driven by what the skin is doing, not by what age a child hits. A 12-year-old with perfectly clear skin doesn't need adapalene just because they turned 12.

## Teaching Skincare as Self-Care, Not Appearance Obsession

![A parent teaching a child to wash their face](/images/parent-teaching-skincare.jpg)

There's a fine line between teaching good hygiene and creating appearance anxiety. How you introduce skincare matters as much as what products you buy.

Frame skincare as body maintenance, same as brushing teeth or washing hands. "We take care of our skin because it protects us" lands differently than "we need to fix your skin." One is neutral and health-oriented. The other implies there's something wrong.

Don't link skincare to attractiveness. "You'll have such nice skin if you wash your face" connects skin to appearance in a way that can become obsessive. "Washing your face keeps your skin healthy and comfortable" is factually accurate without the beauty-standard baggage.

Let them lead. If your 9-year-old isn't interested in skincare and their skin is fine, don't push it. The routine will become relevant when their skin changes. Forcing it early creates resistance.

Keep your own commentary neutral. "I noticed some new bumps, want me to grab you a face wash?" is straightforward and helpful. "What's happening with your face?" is not.

Research on body image in preadolescents shows that parental comments about appearance, even well-intentioned ones, are among the strongest predictors of body dissatisfaction in children (Rodgers et al., 2016). Be careful with your words around skin.

## Key Takeaways

- Skincare readiness is biological, not calendar-based. Watch for oiliness, first blackheads, and body odor as signals, typically between ages 8-11.
- The starting preteen routine is gentle cleanser + moisturizer. Two products. Don't introduce active ingredients (retinoids, acids, benzoyl peroxide) before they're needed.
- TikTok skincare routines designed for adults can damage preteen skin. Set clear boundaries around specific ingredients and have honest conversations about why.
- Escalate gradually based on what the skin is doing: salicylic acid at 10-11 if needed, adapalene at 12-13 if breakouts persist, dermatologist at any age if acne is severe.
- Frame skincare as body maintenance, not appearance management. The language you use shapes how your child relates to their skin.

## The Bottom Line

Starting a preteen on skincare isn't complicated, but it requires some restraint. The instinct to throw products at the problem, especially when your kid is upset about their first blackheads, is understandable. But preteen skin is young, thin, and still developing its protective barrier. It needs gentleness, not a 7-step routine borrowed from a 25-year-old influencer. A $12 cleanser and a $14 moisturizer will handle most preteen skin situations. When they're not enough, you add one thing at a time, match the treatment to the actual condition, and see a dermatologist if things get beyond what OTC products can manage. The kids who end up with the healthiest skin long-term aren't the ones who started retinol at 10. They're the ones who learned to take care of their skin barrier first and add actives only when their skin actually needed them.
