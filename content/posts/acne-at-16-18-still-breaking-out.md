# Acne at 16–18: Why You're Still Breaking Out and What to Try Next

There's a specific kind of loneliness that comes with being 17 and still breaking out when half your friend group has clear skin. At 14, everyone had acne. It was universal, almost bonding. But by 16 or 17, the playing field splits. Some people's skin just... figured itself out. And some people's didn't.

If your teenager is in the second group, or if you're the teenager reading this yourself, I want to start with the most important thing: persistent acne at 16-18 is not unusual, it's not your fault, and it's very treatable. But the approach that worked (or didn't work) at 14 probably needs to change.

![An older teenager looking frustrated at persistent acne](/images/older-teen-frustrated.jpg)

## Why Acne Persists When Peers Are Clearing Up

The reason some people clear up at 15 while others are still fighting breakouts at 18 comes down to a few factors, and genetics tops the list.

A twin study published in the *British Journal of Dermatology* estimated that 81% of the variance in acne severity was attributable to genetic factors (Bataille et al., 2002). If one or both parents had persistent acne through their late teens, the odds are high their child will too. This isn't about hygiene or diet choices. It's about how your skin responds to hormones, how quickly your pores shed dead cells, and how aggressively your immune system reacts to *Cutibacterium acnes* bacteria.

The hormonal picture at 16-18 also has some specific nuances worth understanding:

**For boys:** Testosterone levels are still climbing through 18-19 in most males, meaning the hormonal driver behind sebum production hasn't peaked yet. A study in the *Journal of Clinical Endocrinology and Metabolism* showed that testosterone levels in boys continue rising steadily through late adolescence, with sebum production rates closely tracking those levels (Lucky et al., 1997). Some guys won't see their acne genuinely start to improve until their early 20s when hormone levels finally plateau.

**For girls:** The picture is more complicated. While adrenal and ovarian androgen production stabilizes earlier in females, the menstrual cycle introduces a monthly hormonal fluctuation that can drive cyclical breakouts. Many teenage girls notice their acne flares in the week before their period. This is driven by a progesterone surge in the luteal phase, which increases sebum production, followed by a drop in estrogen that reduces the skin's anti-inflammatory protection (Lucky, 2004). Girls who started periods late or have irregular cycles may have more persistent acne because their hormonal patterns haven't settled into a rhythm yet.

There's also a condition called polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) that affects an estimated 6-12% of women of reproductive age and frequently shows up during the teenage years. PCOS involves elevated androgen levels and can cause persistent, often jawline-concentrated acne that doesn't respond well to standard topical treatments. Other signs include irregular periods, excess facial or body hair, and weight gain. If your daughter's acne is stubborn and concentrated along the jawline and chin, it's worth bringing up PCOS with her doctor (Azziz et al., 2009).

## The Emotional Weight Nobody Talks About Enough

I'm going to be blunt about this. Being the 17-year-old with acne when your friends have clear skin is genuinely awful.

At 13-14, acne is shared suffering. By 16-18, it becomes isolating. Senior photos are coming. Prom. College visits. First jobs. Dating is more serious. And the unspoken assumption among peers is that acne is something you should have "outgrown" by now, as if it's a choice.

A study in the *Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology* found that older adolescents with acne reported higher levels of social anxiety and avoidance behaviors compared to younger adolescents with similar acne severity (Halvorsen et al., 2011). The researchers hypothesized that the social expectation of clear skin increases with age, making the same condition psychologically harder to bear at 17 than at 13.

I've seen parents underestimate this. "You had acne at 14 and it didn't bother you this much." Right, because at 14 everyone else had it too. Context changes everything.

Some practical things to watch for at this age:

- Declining interest in social activities they used to enjoy
- Excessive time spent on skincare routines (2+ hours daily, constantly touching or examining skin)
- Avoiding eye contact or angling their face away from people
- Resistance to being photographed
- Spending money they don't have on products promising overnight results
- Expressing hopelessness ("nothing works," "I'll never have clear skin")

If you're seeing these patterns, the conversation needs to shift from skincare to support. A dermatologist visit isn't just about clearing skin at this point; it's about mental health.

## When It's Time for Prescription-Strength Treatment

Here's my honest opinion: if a teenager has been dealing with persistent acne from 13 or 14 through to 16-18, and they've been using a proper OTC routine (benzoyl peroxide, adapalene, consistent application for months), they've earned a dermatologist appointment. Not as a last resort. As the logical next step.

![A teenager at a dermatologist appointment](/images/teen-derm-appointment.jpg)

The AAD's guidelines for acne management recommend escalating treatment when first-line topical therapy fails after an adequate trial, typically defined as 12 weeks of consistent use (Zaenglein et al., 2016). For a teenager who has been trying for two or three years, that threshold has long been crossed.

What a dermatologist can offer that OTC products can't:

**Prescription topical retinoids:** Tretinoin (0.025-0.1%) and tazarotene are stronger than OTC adapalene and can be more effective for stubborn comedonal acne. They require a prescription because of stronger side effects (more dryness, peeling, and sun sensitivity), but for resistant acne they're often worth it.

**Topical combination prescriptions:** Products like Epiduo Forte (adapalene 0.3% + benzoyl peroxide 2.5%) or clindamycin/benzoyl peroxide gel deliver multiple actives in a single formulation that's been tested for stability and efficacy.

**Oral antibiotics:** Doxycycline is the most commonly prescribed. It works systemically to reduce *C. acnes* bacteria and has anti-inflammatory properties beyond its antibiotic action. The standard course is 3-6 months. It's meant as a bridge, not a permanent solution, because long-term antibiotic use contributes to resistance (Del Rosso et al., 2016).

**Hormonal treatments (for girls):** Certain combined oral contraceptives (Yaz, Ortho Tri-Cyclen, Beyaz) are FDA-approved for acne. They work by reducing circulating androgens. Spironolactone, an androgen receptor blocker, is used off-label but has strong evidence for hormonal acne in females. These options are worth discussing with a dermatologist and gynecologist, particularly for girls whose acne clearly worsens with their menstrual cycle.

## The Accutane Conversation

I'm devoting a separate section to isotretinoin (most people still call it Accutane, though the brand name was discontinued years ago) because it comes with more questions and anxiety than any other acne treatment.

Here's what isotretinoin actually is: a derivative of vitamin A that targets every major cause of acne simultaneously. It shrinks sebaceous glands by up to 90%, normalizes keratinization inside follicles, reduces *C. acnes* populations, and has anti-inflammatory effects. No other acne medication does all four (Layton, 2009).

The results reflect that. A systematic review in the *Cochrane Database* found that a standard 4-6 month course of isotretinoin produces complete or near-complete clearance in the majority of patients, with relapse rates of roughly 10-20% in studies with long-term follow-up (Costa et al., 2018).

Now for the part everyone worries about: side effects.

**What almost everyone experiences:** Dry lips (often severely), dry skin, dry eyes, nosebleeds, muscle aches, increased sun sensitivity. These are dose-dependent and resolve after stopping the medication.

**What some people experience:** Elevated liver enzymes or cholesterol (monitored through monthly blood tests), headaches, joint pain, temporary acne flare in the first few weeks.

**The serious concern:** The historical association between isotretinoin and depression/suicidality. This has been studied extensively, and the current scientific consensus is less alarming than the internet suggests. A large-scale study of over 5,000 isotretinoin patients published in the *British Medical Journal* found no increased risk of depression or suicide attempt during or after treatment (Sundstrom et al., 2010). Some researchers have actually found that depression scores *improved* during isotretinoin treatment, likely because the acne itself was clearing (Huang & Cheng, 2017).

That said, any teenager on isotretinoin should be monitored for mood changes, not because the drug necessarily causes depression, but because they're teenagers dealing with a chronic skin condition, and depression screening is warranted regardless.

**The iPLEDGE program:** In the US, isotretinoin requires enrollment in iPLEDGE, a risk management program that primarily exists to prevent pregnancy during treatment (isotretinoin causes severe birth defects). For female patients, this means monthly pregnancy tests, two forms of contraception, and monthly prescriber check-ins. It's cumbersome. It's also non-negotiable.

My take on isotretinoin: if your teenager has severe acne, acne that's scarring, or acne that hasn't responded to multiple other treatments over a year or more, isotretinoin is worth serious consideration. The side effect profile sounds intimidating on paper, but for most patients, the experience is manageable dry lips and skin for a few months in exchange for potentially permanent clearance. The alternative, years of ongoing acne with progressive scarring, carries its own very real costs.

## College Prep Skincare: Building Independence

There's a practical dimension to acne at 16-18 that doesn't exist at younger ages: many of these teenagers are about to leave home. Within a year or two, they'll be managing their skin entirely on their own, possibly in a dorm bathroom shared with three other people.

![A teenager's skincare routine on a college dorm desk](/images/dorm-skincare-desk.jpg)

If your teenager is headed to college with active acne, spending some time on skincare independence before they leave is genuinely useful.

**Simplify the routine.** A routine that requires six products applied in a specific order with different morning and evening protocols is going to collapse in a dorm. The ideal college routine is three products, maybe four. Cleanser, treatment, moisturizer, sunscreen. If it takes more than five minutes, it's too complicated for consistent use by someone also managing a class schedule, a social life, and dining hall food.

**Stock up before move-in.** Running out of benzoyl peroxide three weeks into freshman year and not getting around to replacing it for a month is a real scenario. Send them with enough product to last the semester, or set up auto-delivery through Amazon or the brand's website.

**Handle prescriptions early.** If they're on a prescription like tretinoin or doxycycline, figure out the refill logistics before they leave. Can their existing dermatologist send refills to a pharmacy near campus? Do they need to find a new dermatologist? Is there a student health center that can manage refills? These are solvable problems, but not if you wait until they're out of medication.

**Set realistic expectations about dorm life and skin.** Stress, inconsistent sleep, new dietary habits, communal bathrooms, and probably more alcohol than they'll admit to: all of these affect skin. Most college freshmen see some acne fluctuation regardless of their skincare routine. This is normal and temporary. The goal isn't perfection. It's maintaining a basic routine through the chaos.

## Natural Options With Real Evidence

For teenagers who want an alternative to conventional treatments, or parents looking for something gentler as a starting point, there are a handful of natural approaches with genuine research behind them.

Tea tree oil (5%) has demonstrated antibacterial activity against *C. acnes* comparable to benzoyl peroxide, though with slower onset. The classic study in the *Medical Journal of Australia* showed similar efficacy to 5% benzoyl peroxide with fewer side effects (Bassett et al., 1990). The catch: it must be properly diluted, and some people develop contact sensitivity.

Niacinamide (vitamin B3) at 4% concentration reduced inflammatory lesion counts comparably to 1% topical clindamycin in a randomized trial (Shalita et al., 1995). It's available in many OTC moisturizers and serums and causes minimal irritation.

For older teenagers who've been through the conventional product cycle and want something different, or who are dealing with the skin sensitivity that comes from years of benzoyl peroxide and retinoids, their approach is worth checking out. They're not making miracle claims; they're using ingredients that have actual evidence behind them.

Zinc supplements (30-45mg daily of zinc gluconate) showed modest benefit for inflammatory acne in a randomized controlled trial published in *Dermatology*, though the effect was less pronounced than oral antibiotics (Dreno et al., 2001). Worth considering as an adjunct, not a primary treatment.

## Key Takeaways

- Persistent acne at 16-18 is largely genetic and hormonal, not a hygiene failure. Boys may not see improvement until their early 20s; girls should be evaluated for hormonal causes including PCOS.
- The emotional toll of acne increases with age as peers clear up. Watch for social withdrawal, avoidance behaviors, and hopelessness.
- After 12+ weeks of consistent OTC treatment without improvement, a dermatologist appointment is the logical next step, not a last resort.
- Isotretinoin is the most effective acne treatment available. The side effect profile is manageable for most patients, and current research does not support a causal link to depression.
- College-bound teenagers need a simplified, sustainable routine and prescription logistics sorted before they leave home.

## The Bottom Line

Acne at 16-18 feels personal in a way it didn't at 13. When everyone else is clearing up and you're not, it's hard not to internalize that as something wrong with you rather than something wrong with your skin. But persistent late-teen acne is a medical condition with medical solutions. OTC products work for many people, prescriptions work for most of the rest, and isotretinoin works for nearly everyone who needs it. The worst thing you can do is wait and hope, because time isn't neutral here. Every month of untreated inflammatory acne is another month of potential scarring. Get the right treatment, keep it simple enough to actually stick with, and give yourself the same patience you'd give any other health issue that takes time to resolve.
