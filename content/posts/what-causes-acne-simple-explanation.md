# What Actually Causes Acne? A Simple Explanation That Makes Sense

I spent years confused about why my face decided to wage war against me. Every article I read threw around terms like "comedogenesis" and "pilosebaceous unit" as if I had a dermatology degree. I didn't. I just wanted to know why I kept getting pimples when my best friend's skin stayed clear eating the same garbage food as me.

So here's the explanation I wish someone had given me at 14. No jargon, no hedging, just what's actually going on under your skin.

![A simple diagram showing how a pore gets clogged](/images/pore-clog-diagram.jpg)

## Your Skin Is Basically a Factory

You have tiny oil glands attached to almost every hair follicle on your body. These are called sebaceous glands, and their entire job is to pump out an oily substance called sebum. Sebum isn't the enemy. It keeps your skin from drying out and cracking. Without it, you'd look like a lizard shedding its skin 24/7.

Here's how the system is supposed to work: the gland makes sebum, the sebum travels up through the pore, it coats your skin surface, done. Simple plumbing.

Acne happens when that plumbing breaks down. And it breaks down because of four things happening at the same time.

## The Four Factors (All of Them Matter)

### 1. Too Much Sebum

During puberty, your body cranks up production of hormones called androgens. Both guys and girls produce them. Androgens are like a volume knob for your oil glands. They tell those glands to grow bigger and produce more sebum than you actually need.

This is why acne typically shows up between ages 11 and 14. It's not random. It lines up almost perfectly with when androgen levels start climbing. A study published in the *Journal of Clinical and Aesthetic Dermatology* found that sebum production rates in teenagers with acne were measurably higher than in teens without it (Makrantonaki & Zouboulis, 2004).

The oil itself isn't dirty. I want to be clear about that. It's your body doing what it thinks is right, just overdoing it.

### 2. Dead Skin Cells That Won't Leave

Your skin replaces itself constantly. Old cells on the surface die off and are supposed to shed away, making room for new ones. In people prone to acne, this process goes sideways. Instead of shedding normally, dead skin cells inside the pore become sticky and clump together.

Researchers call this "abnormal desquamation," but what it means in plain language is that dead cells pile up inside the pore like leaves clogging a drain. Combined with the excess oil, you get a plug. That plug is either a whitehead (closed) or a blackhead (open to the air, which oxidizes it and turns it dark).

The blackhead color has nothing to do with dirt, by the way. That's a myth that refuses to die.

### 3. Bacteria That Thrive in the Mess

There's a specific bacterium that lives on everyone's skin called *Cutibacterium acnes* (it used to go by *Propionibacterium acnes* until they renamed it). This bug is normally harmless. It hangs out in your pores, eats sebum, and minds its own business.

But when a pore gets plugged up with oil and dead cells, you've created a warm, oxygen-poor, oil-rich environment. For *C. acnes*, that's paradise. The population explodes inside the blocked pore. As the bacteria multiply, they produce enzymes and metabolic byproducts that irritate the surrounding tissue (Dréno et al., 2018).

It's worth noting that newer research suggests this isn't just about how many bacteria are present. The specific strains matter. Some strains of *C. acnes* are more inflammatory than others, which partly explains why two people with similar oil levels can have very different acne (Fitz-Gibbon et al., 2013).

### 4. Inflammation (The Body's Overreaction)

Your immune system notices the bacterial overgrowth and sends inflammatory cells to deal with it. This is where you get the redness, swelling, and pain. The bigger the immune response, the worse the pimple looks.

Mild inflammation gives you a papule (a small red bump). More inflammation produces a pustule (the ones with the white or yellow center that everyone wants to pop). If the follicle wall actually ruptures deep in the skin, you can get nodules or cysts, which are the painful, deep lumps that take forever to go away and can leave scars.

Here's something that surprised me: recent research shows that inflammation actually starts earlier in the process than we used to think. It's not just a reaction to the bacteria. There's evidence that subtle inflammation is present even before the pore gets visibly clogged (Jeremy et al., 2003). Your immune system seems to be involved from the very beginning.

## How These Four Factors Work Together

None of these four things causes acne by itself. You need the combination:

Hormones ramp up oil production. Dead cells stick together inside the pore. The combination creates a plug. Bacteria feast on the trapped oil. Your immune system responds with inflammation.

Think of it like a chain reaction. Remove one link, and you've either prevented acne or at least made it less severe. This is actually the basis for most acne treatments. Retinoids target the dead cell buildup. Benzoyl peroxide kills bacteria. Certain birth control pills reduce androgen-driven oil production. Anti-inflammatories address the immune response.

Understanding this chain is genuinely useful because it helps you figure out which treatments make sense for your specific situation and which ones are nonsense.

## The Oil Gland Lifecycle

Here's something most articles skip over, and I think it matters.

Your sebaceous glands don't just squeeze out oil like a tube of toothpaste. The cells inside the gland actually fill up with lipids (fats) over about two to three weeks. Then they burst open, releasing their contents into the pore. The cell itself dies in the process. New cells form, fill up, burst. Repeat.

This cycle is why acne treatments take weeks to work. You're not just clearing existing blockages. You're waiting for the gland's production cycle to normalize. Anyone who tells you a product will clear acne overnight is either confused or lying.

The composition of your sebum also changes during puberty. Teenage sebum tends to have different ratios of fatty acids and wax esters compared to adult sebum, and some of those components are more likely to trigger inflammation or feed *C. acnes* (Zouboulis et al., 2014). This is another piece of why puberty and acne are so tightly linked.

## Why You Get Acne and Your Friend Doesn't

This is the question that drives people crazy, and the honest answer is genetics. If one or both of your parents had acne, your chances are substantially higher. A large twin study found that genetic factors account for roughly 80% of the variance in acne severity (Bataille et al., 2002). Eighty percent. That's enormous.

What are they inheriting? A few things:

- **How much oil your glands produce.** Some people just have larger, more active sebaceous glands.
- **How your skin cells behave inside the pore.** The tendency for dead cells to clump instead of shedding normally is heritable.
- **How your immune system responds.** Some people's immune systems react more aggressively to *C. acnes*, producing more inflammation from the same bacterial load.
- **Hormone sensitivity.** Two people can have identical androgen levels but different acne severity because their oil glands are more or less sensitive to those hormones.

This doesn't mean acne is purely predetermined. Environmental factors, skincare habits, and treatments all matter. But it explains why you can do everything "right" and still break out, while someone else does nothing and stays clear. It's not fair, and anyone who tells you that you just need to wash your face better is, respectfully, wrong.

## What Does NOT Cause Acne

I want to spend some time here because the myths around acne are relentless, and some of them cause real harm.

### Dirty Skin

Acne is not caused by dirt. It's not caused by not washing your face enough. In fact, over-washing and scrubbing aggressively can damage your skin barrier, increase inflammation, and make acne worse. The American Academy of Dermatology recommends washing your face twice a day with a gentle cleanser and being done with it (AAD, 2024).

The confusion comes from blackheads. People see the dark color and assume it's dirt trapped in the pore. It's not. It's oxidized melanin and sebum. No amount of scrubbing will prevent blackheads from forming because the problem starts inside the pore, not on the surface.

### Chocolate (By Itself)

The chocolate-causes-acne belief has been around since at least the 1930s. The evidence is murky at best. A couple of small studies have suggested that chocolate might have a minor effect on acne in some people, but the studies had real methodology problems: tiny sample sizes, no controls for sugar and dairy (which might be the actual culprits), and short timeframes.

The current scientific consensus is that chocolate alone isn't a meaningful acne trigger for most people. High-glycemic diets in general might play a role (more on that in another post), but singling out chocolate specifically isn't supported by the research (Vongraviopap & Asawanonda, 2016).

### Stress Alone

Stress can worsen existing acne. There's decent evidence for that. Cortisol, the stress hormone, can increase sebum production. But stress by itself, without the other factors in place, doesn't cause acne to appear. If you don't have the genetic predisposition, being stressed won't give you pimples.

A study of college students found that acne severity correlated with exam periods, but the students all had pre-existing acne (Chiu et al., 2003). Stress was making a bad situation worse, not creating a new one.

### Greasy Food

Eating pizza and french fries doesn't cause acne. The grease you eat doesn't teleport to your pores. This myth probably stuck around because greasy food and puberty tend to overlap, but correlation isn't causation. Your diet can influence acne through blood sugar and insulin pathways, but the mechanism is completely different from "grease goes to your face."

## Why This Matters

Understanding what causes acne isn't just academic. It changes how you approach treatment. If you know that the core issue is a combination of oil, dead cells, bacteria, and inflammation, you can:

- Stop blaming yourself for having acne (it's mostly genetics and hormones)
- Stop wasting money on products that target things that don't matter (charcoal face masks for "detoxing," I'm looking at you)
- Have better conversations with a dermatologist because you understand what they're trying to address
- Be more patient with treatments because you know they need to work through the oil gland cycle
- Evaluate new products and advice with a basic filter: does this actually address one of the four factors?

## Key Takeaways

- **Acne is caused by four things working together**: excess oil, sticky dead skin cells, bacterial overgrowth, and inflammation. None of them alone is enough.
- **Genetics account for about 80% of acne risk.** If your parents had it, your odds are high, and that's not your fault.
- **Dirty skin, chocolate, stress alone, and greasy food do not cause acne.** These myths persist but they're not supported by the research.
- **Treatments take weeks to work** because they need to interrupt the oil gland's natural production cycle, not just fix what's on the surface today.
- **Understanding the cause helps you pick better treatments.** Every effective acne treatment targets at least one of the four factors.

## The Bottom Line

Acne is a medical condition driven by hormones, genetics, and the interaction between your oil glands, skin cells, bacteria, and immune system. It's not a hygiene problem. It's not a character flaw. And it's not something you caused by eating the wrong thing at lunch.

The good news is that because we understand the mechanism pretty well, we have treatments that genuinely work. The bad news is that none of them are instant. But knowing what you're actually dealing with is the first step toward handling it effectively.
