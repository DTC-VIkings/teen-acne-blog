# Does Chocolate Cause Acne? What the Research Actually Says

Your mom says chocolate causes pimples. Your dermatologist says diet doesn't matter. Your friend swears she breaks out every time she eats a Snickers bar. Everybody has an opinion on this one, and most of them are wrong in one direction or the other.

![A teenager eating a piece of chocolate and looking guilty](/images/teen-eating-chocolate.jpg)

The honest answer is that the relationship between chocolate and acne is real, probably, for at least some people, but it's not the simple cause-and-effect story that either side wants it to be. I've spent a lot of time reading the actual studies on this, and what strikes me most is how much the science has shifted in the last 15 years. What dermatologists confidently told patients in 2005 ("diet has nothing to do with acne") looks increasingly wrong.

Here's what happened, and what we actually know now.

## The Origin Story: That 1969 Study Everyone Cites

For decades, dermatologists dismissed the chocolate-acne connection, and they did it largely on the basis of one study.

In 1969, Dr. James Fulton and colleagues (including the legendary Dr. Albert Kligman, who invented tretinoin) published a study in *JAMA* that seemed to settle the question [1]. They gave 65 subjects chocolate bars or a "control" bar that looked and tasted similar but contained no chocolate. After four weeks, they found no significant difference in acne between the two groups.

Case closed. Chocolate doesn't cause acne. For the next 40 years, dermatologists repeated this finding as settled science.

There were just a few problems with the study.

**The control bar was arguably worse than the chocolate.** The placebo bar was made largely of partially hydrogenated vegetable oil and had a nearly identical sugar and fat content to the chocolate bar. If you're trying to test whether chocolate causes acne, your control can't be a bar that contains the same amount of sugar and similar fats. That's like testing whether red wine causes headaches by comparing it to white wine with the same alcohol content. You're not testing the thing you think you're testing.

**The sample size was small.** 65 people for four weeks is barely adequate to detect anything, especially given the natural variability of acne.

**Conflict of interest.** The study was funded by the Chocolate Manufacturers Association of the United States. I'm not saying this automatically invalidates the results, but it's worth knowing.

**Outcome measurement was crude.** The researchers counted lesions manually and used broad categories. Modern acne studies use standardized grading scales, photography, and sebum measurements.

Despite these issues, the Fulton study basically shut down chocolate-acne research for four decades. Why study something that's already been "proven" a non-issue?

## Modern Research: The Pendulum Swings Back

Starting around 2011, a new generation of researchers decided to look at this question again, and their findings have been uncomfortable for dermatologists who spent decades telling patients to eat whatever they want.

### The Caperton 2014 Study

In 2014, a team led by Dr. Caroline Caperton at the University of Miami published a small but well-designed study in the *Journal of Clinical and Aesthetic Dermatology* [2]. They gave 14 men with acne-prone skin capsules filled with either unsweetened 100% cocoa powder or gelatin capsules (the placebo) and counted acne lesions at days 4 and 7.

The results were striking. The cocoa group showed a statistically significant increase in acne lesions compared to placebo, and the increase happened within just 4 days. This was pure cocoa, no sugar, no dairy, no added fat. Just chocolate by itself.

The sample size was tiny (14 people), so we shouldn't overstate the conclusion. But it was the first modern study using pure cocoa and a true placebo.

### The Vongraviopap 2016 Study

A study published in the *International Journal of Dermatology* found similar results [3]. Men who consumed 99% dark chocolate showed increases in acne lesions at 2 and 4 weeks compared to baseline. Again, this was very dark chocolate with minimal sugar content.

### The Delost 2016 Study

![A close-up of dark chocolate squares on a table](/images/dark-chocolate-squares.jpg)

Another small trial, this one from Northeast Ohio Medical University, found that consuming 6 ounces of chocolate daily for one week led to a significant increase in acne lesions compared to baseline [4]. They also found increases in specific inflammatory markers in the skin.

### What These Studies Suggest

Something in cocoa itself, independent of sugar and dairy, appears to worsen acne in at least some people. The proposed mechanism involves cocoa's effect on the immune system: chocolate may increase the reactivity of certain immune cells to *Cutibacterium acnes* bacteria, leading to a stronger inflammatory response when bacteria are present [5]. In other words, chocolate might not create new acne so much as turn up the volume on the acne process that's already happening.

But I want to be clear: all of these studies were small (under 30 participants), conducted primarily on men, and looked at short time periods. We don't have a large, long-term, diverse randomized controlled trial. The evidence leans toward "yes, chocolate probably worsens acne," but it's not definitive.

## Dark vs. Milk Chocolate: It's Complicated

You might think dark chocolate would be safer than milk chocolate for acne, since it has less sugar and no dairy. Based on the studies above, that's not necessarily true. The cocoa itself appears to be part of the problem.

However, milk chocolate brings two additional acne triggers that dark chocolate doesn't:

**Dairy.** Multiple large studies have found associations between dairy consumption and acne, particularly skim milk. A 2005 study of over 47,000 women in the *Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology* found that those who reported higher milk consumption as teenagers had significantly more acne [6]. A 2008 follow-up study in boys found similar results [7]. The proposed mechanism involves hormones naturally present in milk, including insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1), which stimulates sebum production and skin cell growth.

Milk chocolate contains dairy. Dark chocolate (70%+ cocoa) typically contains little to no dairy, depending on the brand.

**Sugar.** And here's where we get into the glycemic index angle.

## The Glycemic Index Connection

A milk chocolate bar is essentially three things: cocoa, sugar, and dairy. We've discussed cocoa and dairy. Sugar deserves its own section.

The glycemic index (GI) measures how quickly a food raises blood sugar. High-GI foods (white bread, candy, sugary drinks) cause rapid blood sugar spikes. Your body responds by releasing insulin and IGF-1. Both of these hormones stimulate sebaceous glands to produce more sebum and promote the growth of skin cells that can block pores [8].

A 2007 study in the *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition* put 43 young men on either a high-GI or low-GI diet for 12 weeks [9]. The low-GI group showed significant improvements in acne compared to the high-GI group. This was one of the first well-designed dietary intervention studies to show a clear acne-diet connection.

A standard milk chocolate bar has a GI of about 49 (moderate), while dark chocolate (70%+) has a GI around 23 (low). On the glycemic index front, dark chocolate is clearly better than milk chocolate. But as the cocoa-specific studies above suggest, the glycemic advantage of dark chocolate may be partially offset by the cocoa content itself.

So if you're choosing between the two: dark chocolate is probably the lesser evil, but it's not acne-neutral.

## What About White Chocolate?

White chocolate contains no cocoa solids. It's cocoa butter, sugar, and milk solids. If the cocoa-specific mechanism from the Caperton study is real, white chocolate should theoretically be less problematic on that front. But it still contains sugar and dairy, so it's not getting a free pass.

Nobody has directly studied white chocolate and acne, as far as I can find. This is one of those gaps in the research that feels silly but genuinely hasn't been filled.

## So Should You Stop Eating Chocolate?

![A teenager checking their skin in a mirror the morning after](/images/checking-skin-morning.jpg)

I don't think the evidence supports a blanket "never eat chocolate" recommendation. But I also don't think dermatologists should still be telling patients that diet doesn't matter. The research has moved past that.

Here's my honest read of where things stand:

**The evidence is mixed but tilts toward "yes, chocolate probably worsens acne for some people."** The mechanisms are plausible (immune cell reactivity, glycemic load, dairy hormones), multiple small studies show consistent effects, and the original study that "disproved" the connection was deeply flawed.

**Individual variation is real.** Some people eat chocolate daily and never break out. Others get a pimple within 48 hours of a candy bar. This variation probably has to do with genetic differences in immune reactivity, hormonal sensitivity, and baseline inflammation levels. There's no blood test for this. You'd have to experiment on yourself.

**If you want to test it:** Eliminate all chocolate (and ideally all high-GI foods and dairy) for 4-6 weeks, then reintroduce chocolate alone and watch what happens over the following 7 days. Track your breakouts with daily photos in consistent lighting. One person's experiment isn't science, but it'll tell you something useful about your own skin.

**If you're not willing to quit chocolate entirely:** Dark chocolate (70%+ cocoa) in moderate amounts is probably better than a daily Snickers bar, but don't assume it's harmless just because dermatology blogs say "dark chocolate is fine for acne." The cocoa-specific studies say otherwise.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The 1969 study that "proved" chocolate doesn't cause acne was funded by the chocolate industry and used a flawed control.** Dermatologists relied on it for 40 years. Modern research tells a different story.

2. **Multiple small studies since 2011 show that pure cocoa, even without sugar or dairy, appears to increase acne lesions within days.** The mechanism likely involves immune cell reactivity to acne bacteria.

3. **Milk chocolate hits you three ways: cocoa, dairy, and sugar.** Each has its own independent pathway to worsen acne. Dark chocolate removes two of those three.

4. **High-glycemic diets clearly worsen acne.** This is actually the strongest part of the diet-acne evidence. Reducing sugar intake broadly will probably help your skin more than just cutting chocolate.

5. **The only way to know how chocolate affects YOUR skin is to test it yourself.** Eliminate for 4-6 weeks, reintroduce, and track results with daily photos.

## Bottom Line

Does chocolate cause acne? Probably yes, at least for some people, and the evidence is stronger than most dermatologists acknowledged for decades. The cocoa itself appears to play a role independent of sugar and dairy, though milk chocolate's combination of all three makes it the worst option. Dark chocolate is likely better but not completely off the hook. If acne is something you're actively struggling with, cutting back on chocolate for a few weeks is a low-cost experiment worth running. If it makes no difference for you personally, eat the chocolate. Life is too short to give up things that don't actually affect your skin.
