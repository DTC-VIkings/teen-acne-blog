# 10 Acne Myths Teenagers Still Believe (And What Science Says)

Acne has been around as long as humans have had skin. You'd think we'd have the facts straight by now. We do not.

I regularly see teenagers (and their parents) making decisions based on ideas about acne that were wrong 30 years ago and are still wrong today. Some of these myths are harmless. Others lead to behaviors that actively make acne worse. A few cost people real money chasing solutions that were never going to work.

So here are 10 myths I want to kill, using actual published research instead of "well, my friend said."

![A teenager with a confused expression looking at acne advice on phone](/images/teen-confused-phone.jpg)

## Myth 1: Dirty Skin Causes Acne

This one is the granddaddy of acne myths, and it's responsible for more damaged skin than any of the others on this list.

Acne is not caused by dirt. The dark color of blackheads isn't dirt trapped in pores; it's oxidized sebum and melanin exposed to air. You could wash your face ten times a day and still have acne, because the problem isn't on the surface of your skin. It's happening inside the follicle: excess sebum production driven by hormones, abnormal skin cell shedding inside the pore, bacterial overgrowth, and an inflammatory immune response (Williams et al., 2012).

Washing your face removes surface oil and dead cells, which is useful. But over-washing, which is what this myth encourages, strips the skin barrier and triggers rebound oil production. A study in *Dermatology* found that patients who washed their faces more than twice daily had no improvement in acne and reported significantly more irritation (Choi et al., 2006). Twice daily with a gentle cleanser is the sweet spot. Anything beyond that is counterproductive.

The damage this myth does is real. Teenagers who believe dirty skin causes acne often scrub aggressively, use harsh products, and feel shame about their breakouts, as if acne is evidence of poor hygiene. It isn't. A teenager with acne might have better hygiene than one with clear skin. The difference is hormones and genetics, not soap.

## Myth 2: Sun Exposure Clears Acne

I understand where this one comes from. After a day at the beach, skin often looks temporarily better. The tan masks redness, and UV light does have a short-term anti-inflammatory effect. But the operative word is "short-term."

UV exposure increases skin cell turnover, which initially can help unclog pores. But it also damages the skin barrier, increases post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (those dark marks acne leaves behind), and triggers a rebound effect where the skin thickens and produces more sebum to protect itself. A study in *Journal of Investigative Dermatology* found that UV exposure actually increased comedone formation over time, even though initial inflammation appeared reduced (Otman et al., 2007).

And then there's the long game: UV damage accumulates. Skin cancer risk. Premature aging. Worsened acne scarring. Using the sun as an acne treatment is trading a temporary cosmetic improvement for permanent skin damage. The AAD explicitly recommends against intentional sun exposure for acne management (AAD, 2024).

If you're wondering about light therapy, that's a different thing entirely. Blue light therapy (specific wavelengths, controlled doses) does have some evidence for acne, but that's a clinical treatment, not lying on the beach.

## Myth 3: Toothpaste Works as a Spot Treatment

This myth is so persistent that I've seen it recommended in YouTube comments from accounts with millions of followers.

Toothpaste contains ingredients like baking soda, hydrogen peroxide, and menthol that can dry out a pimple. That's technically true. It also contains sodium lauryl sulfate, fluoride, and various flavoring agents that are irritating to facial skin and were never designed for this purpose. Applying toothpaste to a pimple is essentially applying a chemical irritant to already-inflamed skin.

Dermatologists have addressed this directly. A review in the *Indian Journal of Dermatology* noted that toothpaste application to acne lesions frequently causes contact dermatitis, chemical burns, and hyperpigmentation, particularly in darker skin tones (Shenoi et al., 2019).

A benzoyl peroxide 2.5% spot treatment costs about $8 and is actually formulated for skin. Toothpaste costs less but also isn't formulated for skin. Using it is like using a hammer when you need a screwdriver. Technically, both are tools.

## Myth 4: Popping Pimples Is Fine If You Do It Right

![A teenager scrubbing face too hard with a washcloth](/images/scrubbing-too-hard.jpg)

I'll be honest: I understand the impulse. A whitehead sitting right on the tip of your nose before school photos is not something anyone can be expected to ignore with perfect stoicism. But "popping pimples is fine" as a general philosophy is wrong.

When you squeeze a pimple, you're applying pressure to an already-inflamed, bacteria-filled pocket in your skin. Some of that contents comes out the top. Some of it gets pushed deeper into the surrounding tissue, spreading the infection and inflammation laterally and downward. This is how a single pimple becomes a cluster, and how a superficial lesion becomes a deep, scarring one.

A study in *Dermatologic Surgery* documented that manual extraction of acne lesions by untrained individuals resulted in higher rates of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation and scarring compared to lesions left alone (Tan et al., 2017). Dermatologists and trained estheticians can extract comedones safely using sterile instruments and proper technique. Your fingers in a bathroom mirror are not that.

The one exception I'll grant: if a whitehead is truly superficial (a thin layer of skin over visible white pus, with no surrounding redness or pain), gently pressing with clean hands after a warm shower is unlikely to cause significant damage. But inflammatory papules, nodules, cystic lesions, or anything that hurts when you touch it? Leave it alone. Seriously.

Hydrocolloid pimple patches are a much better option for whiteheads. They draw fluid out overnight without the trauma of squeezing.

## Myth 5: You'll Grow Out of It

"Don't worry, you'll grow out of it." If you have acne and someone has said this to you, I apologize on behalf of everyone who's used this line. It's not always true, and even when it is true, it doesn't help right now.

The statistics: acne does resolve for many people as hormones stabilize in their late teens and early 20s. But a significant percentage of people don't grow out of it. A cross-sectional study in the *Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology* found acne prevalence of 54% in women and 40% in men aged 25-34 (Collier et al., 2008). Adult acne is common, particularly in women.

"You'll grow out of it" also ignores two things. First, untreated acne can scar, and scars are permanent. Every month of "waiting to grow out of it" is another month of potential permanent skin damage. Second, acne at 15 affects quality of life at 15. Telling a teenager to wait four years for their skin to maybe clear up is like telling someone with a broken arm to wait for it to heal on its own. Technically the body can do that. But why would you?

Acne is treatable at every age. Waiting is not a strategy.

## Myth 6: Only Teenagers Get Acne

This is basically the flip side of Myth 5. People think acne is a teen problem that ends at 20. It doesn't.

Adult acne affects up to 50% of women and 25% of men in their 20s and 30s, with some experiencing acne well into their 40s and 50s (Bhate & Williams, 2013). The causes shift somewhat: adult acne in women is often more hormonal, concentrated along the jawline and chin, and tied to menstrual cycles or conditions like PCOS. But the fundamental process (clogged pores, bacteria, inflammation) is the same.

Why does this myth matter for teenagers? Because it feeds the "you'll grow out of it" narrative, and because it makes adults with acne feel abnormal when they're actually common. It also means that some adults don't seek treatment because they think acne is "supposed to" have ended in high school.

## Myth 7: Greasy Food Causes Acne

Eating a burger and fries does not cause pimples. This is one of the most studied claims in dermatology, and the evidence consistently fails to support a direct link between dietary fat intake and acne.

The confusion comes from a reasonable-sounding logic: greasy food = oily skin = acne. But dietary fat doesn't translate directly to sebum production. Sebum production is driven by hormones (mainly androgens), not by the fat content of your lunch.

That said, diet isn't completely irrelevant. There is moderate evidence linking high-glycemic-index foods (sugar, white bread, processed carbs) to increased acne severity. A meta-analysis in the *Journal of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics* found that high-glycemic diets were associated with worsened acne, likely because blood sugar spikes increase insulin and insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1), which in turn increase androgen activity and sebum production (Burris et al., 2013).

There's also a weaker association between dairy consumption (particularly skim milk) and acne (Adebamowo et al., 2005). The mechanism is unclear but may involve hormones naturally present in milk.

But the pizza-face thing? That's a myth. The grease on a pizza slice isn't going to show up as a pimple the next morning. If diet affects your acne at all, it's through hormonal pathways over weeks, not through direct grease-to-pore transfer overnight.

## Myth 8: More Products = Better Results

This is the myth the skincare industry would prefer you believe, since it directly increases their revenue.

In reality, most dermatologists recommend 3-4 products for acne management: a cleanser, a treatment (retinoid or benzoyl peroxide), a moisturizer, and sunscreen. That's it. Every product added beyond what's necessary increases the chance of irritation, allergic reaction, and ingredient interactions that reduce effectiveness.

I've seen teenagers with 8, 10, even 12 products in their routine. Serums layered on serums. A glycolic acid toner followed by a salicylic acid serum followed by a retinoid. That's not skincare; that's a chemistry experiment performed on live tissue.

More products also means more potential for comedogenic ingredients. Every cream, serum, or oil that touches your face contains a list of ingredients, and any one of them could clog pores in your specific skin. The more products you use, the more variables you introduce, and the harder it becomes to identify what's helping and what's causing problems.

A study in the *Journal of Drugs in Dermatology* found that streamlined acne regimens (3-4 products) had equal or better clinical outcomes compared to more complex routines, with significantly better patient adherence (Del Rosso, 2014). Simpler routines are easier to stick with, and consistency beats complexity every time.

## Myth 9: Expensive Products Work Better

A $60 cleanser does not clean your face better than a $12 one. This is not my opinion. This is chemistry.

Facial cleansers work by using surfactants to emulsify oil and allow it to be rinsed away with water. The surfactants in a luxury cleanser are functionally identical to the surfactants in a drugstore cleanser. You're paying for packaging, marketing, brand positioning, and sometimes fragrance. Not for better cleaning.

The same applies to most product categories. CeraVe Moisturizing Cream ($16 for 19oz) contains ceramides, hyaluronic acid, and cholesterol in a well-formulated base. A luxury moisturizer at $80 for 1.7oz might contain the same core ingredients with some additional extracts that sound impressive but have minimal evidence behind them.

Where price can matter: prescription medications (which have specific concentrations of proven active ingredients), professional treatments (which require trained practitioners and specialized equipment), and certain formulations where stability or delivery technology genuinely differs. But for basic cleansing, moisturizing, and OTC acne treatment, drugstore products perform as well as prestige brands in head-to-head studies.

The AAD's recommended acne regimen can be assembled entirely from drugstore products for under $50 total, with each product lasting 2-3 months. That's roughly $20/month for a dermatologist-backed routine. You don't need to spend $200.

## Myth 10: Acne Means You're Not Washing Enough

I saved this for last because it's really Myth 1 wearing a slightly different hat, and because it's the one that causes the most unnecessary shame.

Acne is a medical condition caused by a combination of genetic susceptibility, hormonal activity, bacterial colonization, and inflammatory immune response. It is not caused by insufficient washing. A person can have impeccable hygiene and terrible acne, or terrible hygiene and clear skin. The correlation between washing frequency and acne severity is weak to nonexistent in the research literature (Williams et al., 2012).

![A teenager with clear skin smiling confidently](/images/clear-skin-confident.jpg)

The social implication of this myth is what bothers me most. When people believe acne is caused by not washing, they're essentially saying that people with acne are dirty. That's not just scientifically wrong; it's harmful. Teenagers already struggle with acne-related self-esteem. Adding shame about perceived dirtiness makes it worse.

If you're a teenager reading this and you've ever felt embarrassed because you think people assume you don't wash your face: they're wrong. Your acne isn't about how clean you are. It's about your hormones, your genetics, and your immune system. Those aren't things you can wash away.

## The One Truth Behind Every Myth

There's a pattern running through all of these. Each myth reduces acne to a simple cause-and-effect: dirty skin, wrong food, not enough products, not enough washing. And each of those simple explanations implies that acne is the sufferer's fault. If only you washed more. If only you ate better. If only you used the right products.

Acne isn't that simple, and it isn't anyone's fault. It's a multifactorial skin condition with genetic, hormonal, microbial, and immunological components. Some of those are modifiable (you can treat the bacteria, reduce the inflammation, normalize cell turnover). Some aren't (you can't change your genetics or choose different hormones).

What you can do is stop wasting time and money on approaches based on myths and start using treatments that actually address the real mechanisms behind acne. The evidence base is solid. Benzoyl peroxide, retinoids, and proper cleansing/moisturizing work because they target the actual causes. Toothpaste, aggressive scrubbing, and sunbathing don't work because they don't.

## Key Takeaways

- Acne is a hormonal and genetic condition, not caused by dirt, poor hygiene, or greasy food. Over-washing and scrubbing make it worse, not better.
- Sun exposure, toothpaste, and aggressive pimple-popping all cause more harm than good despite persistent popularity.
- You may not "grow out of" acne, and waiting passively risks permanent scarring. Treat it now with evidence-based products.
- Expensive products don't outperform drugstore options for basic acne care. A complete OTC routine costs under $50.
- More products don't equal better results. Stick to 3-4 products used consistently rather than a 10-step routine that changes weekly.

## The Bottom Line

Most of what teenagers believe about acne comes from other teenagers, social media, or well-meaning adults who are working from outdated information. The result is a lot of wasted effort, unnecessary shame, and skin that's worse off than if they'd just done nothing. The science on acne is actually pretty clear at this point. Use a gentle cleanser twice a day. Apply a retinoid or benzoyl peroxide as your treatment. Moisturize. Wear sunscreen. See a dermatologist if that doesn't work after three months. Skip the toothpaste, stop scrubbing like you're trying to sand down a deck, and please stop believing that your acne is your fault. It isn't. It's biology, and biology responds to treatment, not to guilt.
