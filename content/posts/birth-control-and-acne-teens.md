# Birth Control and Acne: What Older Teens Should Know

I want to start with something that should be obvious but apparently isn't: this article is not medical advice. It's information. If you're a teenager thinking about birth control for acne, that is a conversation between you, your parent or guardian, and a doctor. Not between you and a blog post.

With that out of the way, let's talk about what birth control actually does to acne, because there's a lot of confusion out there and some of it leads to bad decisions.

![A teenager discussing options with a doctor](/images/teen-doctor-discussion.jpg)

## The Hormone Connection

Acne in teenagers is driven largely by androgens. These are hormones that both males and females produce, though males produce more of them. The most relevant ones for acne are testosterone and its more potent form, dihydrotestosterone (DHT).

Here's what they do: androgens bind to receptors on your oil glands and tell them to grow larger and produce more sebum. More sebum means more clogged pores. More clogged pores means more acne. The chain is straightforward.

Certain combination birth control pills (the ones containing both estrogen and a progestin) interrupt this process in two ways:

**First**, the estrogen component increases production of a protein called sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG) in your liver. SHBG circulates in your blood and binds to free testosterone, essentially deactivating it. Less free testosterone means less stimulation of oil glands. A study in *Contraception* found that combined oral contraceptives increased SHBG levels by 200-400%, with corresponding decreases in free testosterone (Zimmerman et al., 2014).

**Second**, some progestins in birth control pills have anti-androgenic properties. This means they actively block androgens from binding to receptors on the oil gland. Not all progestins do this, though. Some are actually androgenic, meaning they mimic testosterone's effects. The type of progestin in the pill matters enormously, and this is where people get tripped up.

## Which Pills Are FDA-Approved for Acne

The FDA has specifically approved three combination oral contraceptives for treating acne:

**Yaz (drospirenone/ethinyl estradiol)** - Contains drospirenone, which is a progestin with strong anti-androgenic activity. It's derived from spironolactone (more on that later). Yaz was approved for acne treatment in 2006. Multiple randomized controlled trials showed it reduced inflammatory and non-inflammatory acne lesions compared to placebo (Maloney et al., 2008).

**Ortho Tri-Cyclen (norgestimate/ethinyl estradiol)** - Contains norgestimate, a progestin with low androgenic activity. It was the first oral contraceptive approved by the FDA for acne, back in 1997. The triphasic formulation means the hormone doses change over the course of the month. Studies showed a roughly 50% reduction in inflammatory lesions over six cycles (Lucky et al., 1997).

**Estrostep (norethindrone acetate/ethinyl estradiol)** - Also a triphasic pill. Norethindrone acetate is technically an androgenic progestin, but the estrogen component in Estrostep is high enough relative to the progestin dose that the overall effect is still anti-androgenic. Approved for acne in 1997.

Other combination pills that aren't FDA-approved for acne can still help with acne. The approval just means those three went through the specific clinical trials required for that label. Many dermatologists prescribe other pills off-label with good results, particularly those containing desogestrel or drospirenone.

## The Timeline (This Is Where People Get Frustrated)

Birth control pills do not clear acne fast. I cannot emphasize this enough because the expectation mismatch causes a lot of people to quit too early.

Here's a realistic timeline based on clinical trial data:

- **Month 1-2**: Often no visible improvement. Some people actually break out worse initially as hormones adjust. This is normal but deeply annoying.
- **Month 3**: Modest improvement for some. Studies typically show the first statistically measurable differences around this point.
- **Month 4-6**: This is where most people see meaningful results. The studies supporting FDA approval for Yaz, Ortho Tri-Cyclen, and Estrostep all measured outcomes at the 6-month mark (Arowojolu et al., 2012).
- **Month 6+**: Continued improvement. Some women don't see full results until 9-12 months.

Three to six months of waiting, minimum. That's a long time when you're a teenager staring at your face every morning. Most dermatologists will prescribe a topical treatment (like benzoyl peroxide or a retinoid) alongside the pill to bridge that gap.

## IUDs and Acne: A Complicated Picture

Not all birth control is the same, and this is where things get tricky.

**Hormonal IUDs (like Mirena, Liletta, Kyleena)** release levonorgestrel, which is an androgenic progestin. It works locally in the uterus and systemic hormone levels are much lower than with pills, but some of it does get absorbed into the bloodstream. There are reports of hormonal IUDs worsening acne, and a large cohort study found a small but real increase in acne diagnoses among hormonal IUD users compared to non-users (Barbieri & Mruk, 2020).

The frustrating part: for many people, hormonal IUDs don't cause acne problems at all. It depends on your individual sensitivity. But if you're specifically using birth control to treat acne, a hormonal IUD is probably not the right choice.

**Copper IUDs (ParaGard)** don't contain hormones, so they won't help or hurt acne.

**Progestin-only pills (the "mini-pill")** can go either way depending on the progestin used. Since they lack estrogen, they don't increase SHBG, which means they don't reduce free testosterone levels the way combination pills do. Some can worsen acne.

**The implant (Nexplanon)** releases etonogestrel, a progestin with some androgenic activity. Acne is listed as a common side effect. In clinical trials, about 14% of users reported acne (Darney et al., 2009).

**The shot (Depo-Provera)** uses medroxyprogesterone acetate. Evidence is mixed, but it generally doesn't help with acne and may worsen it for some people.

The pattern is clear: if treating acne is a goal, combination pills with anti-androgenic progestins are the option that reliably helps. Other forms of birth control may be better for contraception but are neutral or counterproductive for skin.

## Spironolactone: The Non-Contraceptive Alternative

Spironolactone is technically a blood pressure medication, but dermatologists have been using it off-label for hormonal acne in women for decades. It works by blocking androgen receptors directly and reducing androgen production.

It's not birth control. It doesn't prevent pregnancy. In fact, it can cause birth defects, so anyone taking it who is sexually active needs to be on reliable contraception.

For older teens with hormonal acne who either can't take estrogen-containing pills or whose acne isn't responding to them, spironolactone is a legitimate option. Doses for acne typically range from 50-200 mg daily. A large retrospective study found that about 66% of women treated with spironolactone for acne reported improvement, with higher doses showing better results (Charny et al., 2017).

Side effects include increased urination (it's a diuretic), possible lightheadedness, breast tenderness, and irregular periods. Your doctor will want to check your potassium levels periodically since spironolactone can raise them.

It takes time to work too. Similar to birth control pills, expect 2-3 months for initial improvement and up to 6 months for full effect.

## Side Effects Teens Should Actually Think About

Every medication has side effects, and birth control pills are no exception. Here are the ones worth knowing about:

**Common and usually temporary**: nausea, headaches, breast tenderness, mood changes, breakthrough bleeding. These often improve after the first 2-3 months as your body adjusts.

**Weight**: the belief that birth control causes major weight gain is mostly a myth. Large studies show an average gain of 1-2 pounds over a year, which is within normal fluctuation. Drospirenone-containing pills (like Yaz) can actually cause slight weight loss initially because drospirenone has mild diuretic properties (Lopez et al., 2014).

**Blood clots**: this is the serious one. Combination birth control pills slightly increase the risk of blood clots (venous thromboembolism). The absolute risk is still very low for teenagers. Without the pill, the risk is about 1-5 per 10,000 women per year. With the pill, it's about 3-9 per 10,000 per year. For context, the risk during pregnancy is 5-20 per 10,000 (Lidegaard et al., 2011). Drospirenone-containing pills like Yaz carry a slightly higher clot risk than pills with older progestins, which is worth discussing with your doctor.

**Mood**: this is genuinely complicated. Some studies suggest hormonal contraceptives increase the risk of depression in adolescents. A large Danish study found a higher rate of first-time antidepressant prescriptions among teenage pill users compared to non-users (Skovlund et al., 2016). Other studies have not found this association. I think the honest take is that mood effects are real for some people, unpredictable, and worth monitoring.

**What happens when you stop**: if you're taking the pill specifically for acne, the acne often comes back when you stop. The pill manages the hormonal component but doesn't cure the underlying tendency. This is something worth understanding up front so you're not blindsided.

## Who Should Not Take Combination Birth Control Pills

There are clear medical contraindications. You should not take combination pills if you:

- Have a history of blood clots or clotting disorders
- Smoke (especially over age 35, but honestly this applies to teens too)
- Have certain types of migraines with aura
- Have uncontrolled high blood pressure
- Have certain liver conditions
- Have breast cancer or certain other hormone-sensitive cancers

This is one of many reasons why a doctor visit is non-negotiable. They'll screen for these factors and review your family history.

## Having the Conversation with Your Doctor

If you're thinking about birth control for acne, here are things worth bringing up at the appointment:

- How long you've had acne and what you've already tried
- Whether your acne seems hormonal (flares around your period, concentrated along the jawline and chin)
- Any family history of blood clots or cardiovascular issues
- Other medications you're taking (including supplements)
- Your mental health history
- Whether you also need contraception (it's okay to have multiple reasons)

A good doctor will not judge you for asking about this. If they do, find a different doctor. Managing acne is a legitimate medical reason for birth control, and the FDA agrees.

## What I Think Gets Lost in This Conversation

The framing around birth control and acne tends to be either too casual ("just go on the pill!") or too scary ("hormones are dangerous!"). Both extremes miss the point.

The reality is that hormonal birth control is a genuinely effective treatment for hormonal acne, backed by decades of research and used by millions of people. It also has real side effects and isn't right for everyone. These two facts coexist comfortably.

What bugs me is when teenagers with moderate-to-severe hormonal acne aren't even told this is an option, or when they're made to feel like wanting clear skin is frivolous. Acne causes real psychological harm. Studies consistently show it's associated with anxiety, depression, and reduced quality of life in teenagers (Halvorsen et al., 2011). Treating it isn't vanity.

At the same time, birth control isn't the only option and isn't always necessary. Topical treatments work for a lot of people. Oral antibiotics are another route. Isotretinoin (Accutane) exists for severe cases. Birth control is one tool, and it works best for a specific type of acne (hormonally driven) in a specific population (people who can safely take estrogen).

## Key Takeaways

- **Combination birth control pills reduce acne** by lowering free testosterone and, in some formulations, directly blocking androgen receptors on oil glands.
- **Three pills are FDA-approved for acne**: Yaz, Ortho Tri-Cyclen, and Estrostep. Others may work but haven't gone through acne-specific trials.
- **Hormonal IUDs, the implant, and the shot can potentially worsen acne** because they contain progestins without the balancing effect of estrogen.
- **Results take 3-6 months minimum.** Initial worsening is common and doesn't mean the treatment isn't working.
- **This is a decision to make with a doctor,** full stop. Not with a blog, not with a friend, not with TikTok.

## The Bottom Line

Birth control can be a genuinely effective treatment for hormonal acne in older teens. The mechanism is well understood, the evidence is solid, and for the right person, it can make a real difference. But it's a medical decision with real considerations around side effects, contraindications, and expectations. If you think hormonal acne is what you're dealing with, bring it up with a healthcare provider. They can help you figure out whether this approach makes sense for your specific situation or whether a different path would serve you better.
